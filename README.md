🚀 Data Science Summer Internship — Project 1: Business Problem Framing
One of the most underrated skills in Data Science isn't building models — it's framing the right problem. This week's project pushed me to think like a Data Scientist before writing a single line of code.
🔍 The Business Problem: Customer Churn
Most subscription-based businesses lose revenue not because of bad products, but because they don't catch unhappy customers in time. I picked customer churn as my problem statement — a challenge faced by telecom, SaaS, OTT, and banking companies alike.
📌 Converting it into a Data Science Problem
A vague goal like "reduce churn" needs to become something measurable and solvable. I broke it down into:
Business Objective:
Identify customers likely to stop using the service in the next 30 days, so the retention team can intervene early with offers or support.
Required Data:
Customer demographics (age, tenure, location)
Usage/activity patterns (login frequency, transactions, support tickets)
Billing & subscription history (plan type, payment delays, upgrades/downgrades)
Historical churn labels (who churned in the past)
Expected Analytical/ML Output:
A binary classification model predicting churn probability (0–1) for each customer, along with the top features driving that risk — so the business gets both a prediction and an explanation.
💡 Key Learning:
A business problem only becomes solvable once you translate "we're losing customers" into "predict which customers, using what data, to drive what action." Framing it wrong means even the best model won't create real business value.
